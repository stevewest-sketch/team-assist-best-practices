Team Assist Best Practices

A practical guide to setting up, prompting, and running Team Assist: the buttons to configure, the prompts to type, and the plays that put them together. Use it to resolve the customer's issue on the first conversation. Treat it as a conversation, not a search box.

The button library  ·  the prompt library  ·  the plays
Why it matters

A fast reply isn't the goal. A reply that holds is.

A quick answer is easy. The harder thing is a reply that resolves the issue, so the customer doesn't come back tomorrow with the same problem. Used well, Team Assist helps you gather the context, find the right answer, and shape that reply.

Here's the shift: tell it the situation, ask for exactly what you need, and refine from there. The prompts in this guide are written to keep the result specific, instead of returning more than you asked for.

Layer 2 — the prompt library

The exact prompts to type, and what each returns.

Type each one start to finish and fill in the bracketed part. The trick is to be specific: name the scope, the exact dimensions you want, and the length. Team Assist pulls a lot of context, and telling it how to distill is what turns a data dump into something you can use.

Pull broad, but always distill. When you ask for a customer's full history, say what to extract (common contact reasons, what they like and don't like, typical sentiment) and how long (three takeaways, or two to three sentences). Without that, a big pull comes back as a wall of text.

Summarizing

Summarize this customer's history, but only include what's related to [topic].
History filtered to that one topic.
Catch me up on this customer's conversations from the last [30] days.
A window wider than this thread, narrower than the full history.
What has changed with this customer since our last conversation?
What's changed since they last reached out, without re-summarizing everything.
What's the customer's most recent message, and is everything earlier in this conversation still relevant to it?
An answer anchored to the latest message.
Summarize this entire conversation in detail. Don't skip or shorten any part of it.
A complete pass, for when a summary came back thin.
Show me only the order history relevant to what the customer is asking about right now.
Order and account history filtered to the live question.

When multiple answers come back. Team Assist sometimes surfaces several answers across a session with no way to drop them into your reply. Instead of copy-pasting, type: Draft a reply that covers all the answers and context you provided me. The draft, and the Use button, appear once you ask.

Across any play

A few habits worth keeping.

Translate in either direction

Translate the customer's most recent message into English to read one; Translate my reply into [language] before I send it to send one.

Name the shift out loud

When the topic changes mid-conversation, say so directly. Tell it the customer is now asking about a price match, not the return, rather than assuming it caught the shift.

Point by position

When you get more than one option back, use the tone from the first one, but shorter beats re-describing what you want.

A new thread per conversation

Keeps context clean and makes it easy to see where Team Assist was used.

Where to start

Build the habit on the fast path first.

Confirm which Answers are assigned to the Team Assist audience, and configure the Layer 1 buttons so no one is typing them from memory. Then train on the standard fast path. It's the quickest way to make reaching for Team Assist the default instead of the workaround. Introduce the plays one at a time, starting with the conversation your team handles most.

The takeaway. Gather the context, find the right answer, shape the reply, and resolve it the first time, whatever the customer brings you.
